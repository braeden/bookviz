# Poster-style visualization ideas

Same spirit as *every color in Moby-Dick*: one dead-simple rule applied exhaustively to a whole work, rendered as a typographic poster. Grouped by medium; ★ = especially good fit for this codebase.

## Books (text is the data)

- ★ **Every color in …** — this repo.
- ★ **Every sound in …** — onomatopoeia and sound words (*crash, whisper, thunder, creak*), sized by loudness.
- ★ **Every body part in …** — *eyes, hands, heart, blood* in order; anatomy of a novel.
- ★ **Every number in …** — every numeral and written-out number, in order. *Twenty Thousand Leagues* would be great.
- **Every question in …** — only the sentences ending in `?`. Dostoevsky posters would be enormous; Hemingway's tiny.
- **Every "said" in …** — every dialogue tag (*said, cried, whispered, exclaimed*), colored by emotion. Shows an author's dialogue temperature.
- **Every name-drop in …** — each character name in order of mention, one color per character. You see who dominates each act, and exactly when characters enter and exit.
- **Every weather report in …** — rain, fog, sun, storms in order; the emotional barometer of the book (Wuthering Heights = mostly storm).
- **Every food & drink in …** — Dickens or Proust as a menu.
- **Every negation in …** — *no, never, nothing, nobody* — the poster gets darker as tragedy approaches.
- **Sentence-length skyline** — one thin bar per sentence, height = word count; a city skyline of the author's rhythm. (Kafka: long plateaus; Hemingway: flat lowlands.)
- **Punctuation only** — strip every letter, keep the punctuation, flow it as a paragraph. Famous version exists for classic novels; instantly recognizable authors.
- **The heartbeat poster** — em-dashes and exclamation marks per page as an EKG line.
- **First words / last words** — the first word of every chapter set as a poem.

## Movies (scripts + frames are the data)

- ★ **Every color named in the screenplay** — same pipeline, run on public domain scripts.
- **The movie barcode** — every frame squeezed to a 1-pixel column; two hours of color grading in one strip. (Classic form — *Mad Max: Fury Road* is orange/teal proof.)
- **Every F-bomb in …** — timestamped ticks on a timeline; *The Wolf of Wall Street* poster is basically solid ink.
- **Every death in …** — one mark per on-screen death, sized by how it happened; action franchises as war memorials.
- **Dialogue share sunburst** — who speaks when: each act a ring, colored by character. Shows the Ripley takeover in *Alien*.
- **Every location card in …** — INT./EXT. sluglines in order, colored day/night. The movie's floor plan.
- **Shot-length rhythm** — one bar per cut; Michael Bay vs. Tarkovsky on one wall.
- **Every "I love you"** — the line, every time it's said in a rom-com corpus, colored by whether it's reciprocated.

## TV (episodes give you a grid for free)

- ★ **Every color in every episode title** — for shows with formula titles.
- **The catchphrase matrix** — episodes × catchphrase count ("D'oh!", "How you doin'?", "That's what she said") as a heatmap; seasons where writers leaned on the crutch glow hot.
- **Cold-open body count** — one row per season, one cell per episode, mark per death/case/monster; procedurals become quilts.
- **Who's-in-the-room grid** — episodes × characters, cell filled if they appear; you see exactly when the cast rotated (*The Office* post-Michael cliff).
- **IMDb-rating skyline** — every episode a bar, height = rating: the *Game of Thrones* season 8 cliff, the *Breaking Bad* staircase.
- **Every "Previously on…"** — which scenes get recapped most across a series = what the writers think matters.
- **Laugh-track density** — laughs per minute per episode, sitcoms as seismographs.
- **The theme-song wave** — waveform of the intro sized by how many times you've collectively heard it (episodes × viewers).

## Cross-medium series ideas

- Same rule, many works: *every color in* each of the 20 most-downloaded Gutenberg books, printed as a set — the palettes alone identify the books (Moby-Dick is white, Gatsby green and gold, Dracula red and black).
- One author's lifetime: every color in every Dickens novel, chronological — does an author's palette age?
